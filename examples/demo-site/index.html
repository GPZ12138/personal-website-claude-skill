👋 About Me

I'm a Research Engineer at Helix AI, working on post-training reinforcement learning for large language models. My focus is verifiable reasoning — training models with reward signals that can be checked programmatically rather than by a learned critic — and multi-turn agentic RL, where a model has to compose tools, plan, and check its own work over dozens of steps.

Before Helix, I was a Research Intern at Meta FAIR (summer 2024) on long-context post-training for code agents, and at Google DeepMind (summer 2023) on preference-based fine-tuning for the Gemini family. I completed my M.S. in Computer Science at UC Berkeley in 2024 — advised by Prof. M. Chen in the Berkeley AI Research Lab — and my B.S. in CS with a minor in Math at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign.

My belief: the next decade of real economic output depends on systems that can check their own work and act reliably across hundreds of steps. Single-turn chat is the easy part — the hard part is agents that stay honest at scale.

    Process Reward Models for Multi-turn Agentic Reasoning

    Alex Morgan, K. Zhang, J. Park, et al. · Helix AI Technical Report · 23 citations

    First-author paper introducing a family of process reward models for multi-turn agents. Beats outcome-only rewards by ~14% on tool-use benchmarks; uses the same wall-clock training budget.

  2. NeurIPS 2025 ★

    Verifiable Reasoning via Programmatic Rewards

    Alex Morgan, M. Chen, J. Lee · NeurIPS 2025 · 89 citations

    Outstanding Paper runner-up. A training recipe that uses executable verifiers for reasoning tasks. Closes roughly 60% of the gap between pass@1 and pass@16 on MATH, and transfers to code and formal proofs without a learned reward model.

    [Proceedings] [Code] [Talk]

    K. Zhang, Alex Morgan, S. Park, M. Chen · ICML 2025 · 41 citations

  4. arXiv 2025 ★

    Tool-Use Scaling Laws for Foundation Models

    Alex Morgan, S. Park, T. Nguyen · arXiv preprint · 54 citations

    First scaling-law study of tool-use success across model sizes and context lengths. Identifies a clean power-law fit for browser-agent tasks; a significantly noisier one for long-horizon planning.

  5. ICLR 2024 ★

    On the Sample Complexity of Preference-based Fine-tuning

    Alex Morgan, T. Nguyen · ICLR 2024 · 67 citations

    Tight bounds on how many preference pairs a model actually needs to converge under different loss formulations. The log-odds loss is sample-optimal under a mild regularity condition we introduce.

  6. NeurIPS 2024

    Multi-turn Reward Hacking and How to Prevent It

    J. Park, Alex Morgan, M. Chen · NeurIPS 2024 · 38 citations

    Documents three new categories of reward hacking that only emerge in multi-turn interaction, plus a lightweight verifier-ensemble fix.
